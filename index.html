Prologue · Where is Kargil?

A wound in the High Himalayas

The Kargil district sits at the western tip of Ladakh, pressed against the Line of Control separating Indian and Pakistani administered Kashmir. At over 2,600 metres on the valley floor, Kargil is already higher than most European mountain peaks.

And yet the valleys here are the low ground. The ridges above them rise to 5,000–5,600 metres. This vertical gap — between road and ridgeline — is where the war was fought.

Act I · The Line of Control

A boundary that cuts through cloud

The Line of Control — drawn after the 1972 Simla Agreement — does not follow logic. It follows where soldiers stopped fighting. In Kargil, it cuts across ridgelines at 4,000–5,000m, leaving peaks on both sides of an invisible, heavily contested boundary.

In winter 1998–99, Pakistani forces crossed this line and occupied peaks on the Indian side. Not by armour or air, but by small teams climbing in the dark, using altitude itself as cover.

Act II · The Strategic Picture

Peaks above. Highway below.

National Highway 1A — the only road connecting Leh and Ladakh to the rest of India — runs through the valley at 3,000–3,500m, directly under the occupied ridgelines. From Tiger Hill, Pakistani soldiers could observe and fire directly onto the highway. The road existed. It was simply a kill zone.

Act III · The Physics of Height

Looking down vs. looking up

The defender at 5,500m sees 40–50km in every direction. The valley is a stage. Every vehicle, every column is visible. There is nowhere to hide.

The attacker at 3,100m sees only a wall of rock. The ridgeline is silhouetted against sky. Defenders are invisible until they fire. Altitude creates radical information asymmetry — one side knows everything; the other knows almost nothing.

Act IV · Reclamation · Tololing

Tololing: 45 days for one ridge

Tololing (4,590m) commanded the entire Drass valley. India's assault began 13 May 1999. Twelve failed attempts followed. On 13 June, the 13 JAK Rifles finally raised the tricolour — after 45 days of battle on near-vertical rock, at night, under direct fire, at −15°C.

Captain Vikram Batra called the ridge "the staircase to heaven." Every hundred metres gained was bought with blood.

Act IV · Reclamation · Tiger Hill

Tiger Hill: The highest stakes

At 5,589m, Tiger Hill was the war's crown. It overlooked Drass, the highway, and the entire sector. India deployed Bofors howitzers at near-maximum elevation angles. Helicopters could barely function in the thin air. Soldiers melted snow for water.

8 Sikh Regiment recaptured the summit on 4 July 1999 in a night assault that became the war's turning point. To climb Tiger Hill was to fight three enemies: the defender, the altitude, and the cold.

Epilogue · 26 July 1999

Altitude reclaimed — at great cost

Operation Vijay concluded on 26 July 1999 — now Kargil Vijay Diwas. All occupied peaks within Indian territory were recaptured. The highway reopened. The LOC was restored.

The war confirmed what has always been true of mountain warfare: altitude is not terrain. It is a weapon. It multiplies firepower, controls information, chokes supply, and breaks the body. In Kargil, geography did not frame the war — it fought it.

"The mountains are not obstacles to be overcome. They are the architects of the battle plan."
— On the strategic geography of the Kargil War
